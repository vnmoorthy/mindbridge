# Grounding Through Flashbacks & Hyperarousal

## What's happening, and why it's common

If you've served, your body learned to stay ready. That training and those experiences don't just switch off when you come home. A smell, a sound, a crowded room, or a bad night's sleep can suddenly drop you back into a moment that already happened — a flashback — or leave you wound tight, on edge, heart pounding, scanning for threats that aren't there. That's called hyperarousal. It is your nervous system doing what it was conditioned to do to keep you alive. It is not weakness, and it is not "crazy." A lot of veterans living with PTSD experience this. You're not broken, and you're not alone in it.

Grounding is the skill of gently bringing yourself back to the present — reminding your body that right now, in this room, you are safe. It won't erase the past, but it can shorten the moment and give you back some control. Here are things people actually use.

## A few things to try

- **5-4-3-2-1, through your senses.** Slowly name **5** things you can see, **4** you can hear, **3** you can touch, **2** you can smell, and **1** you can taste. Say them out loud if you can. This pulls your attention out of the memory and back into the room you're standing in.

- **Slow the exhale.** Try box breathing — in for 4, hold for 4, out for 4, hold for 4 — or simply make your out-breath longer than your in-breath. A long, slow exhale is one of the fastest ways to signal your body that the threat has passed.

- **Feet on the floor, hands on something real.** Press your feet flat and feel the ground hold you up. Grip a chair, a countertop, your keys. Cold can help too — hold an ice cube, splash cold water on your face, or step outside. Strong physical sensation crowds out the flashback.

- **Orient to now.** Out loud, state the facts: today's date, where you are, how old you are, that you are safe. A steadying line helps: "That was then. This is now. I made it out." Look around and find proof you're in the present.

- **Move.** Stand up, stretch, walk it off, roll your shoulders, push against a wall. Motion tells a keyed-up body it can discharge the energy instead of holding it.

## When to reach out for real support

I'm a companion, not a therapist or a doctor — and some things deserve real human help. If the flashbacks, sleeplessness, or edginess are wearing you down, please talk to a professional. VA offers PTSD care that works, including talk therapies like prolonged exposure and CBT, and you can start at **ptsd.va.gov**. Peer support — other veterans who get it — helps too.

And if you're in crisis, thinking about hurting yourself, or things feel unsafe right now, reach out immediately: **the Veterans Crisis Line — dial 988 then press 1, or text 838255.** It's free, confidential, and staffed 24/7. You don't have to carry this by yourself.
