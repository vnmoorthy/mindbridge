# Moral Injury, Guilt & Shame

## What's happening, and why it's so common

If you're carrying something heavy — something you did, something you didn't do, something you saw, or an order or betrayal that cut against everything you believe in — you are not broken, and you are not alone. There's a name for this. It's often called **moral injury**: the deep wound that comes when events collide with your own sense of right and wrong. It can show up as guilt ("I should have done more"), shame ("what I did makes me a bad person"), grief, or a slow-burning anger.

This is not weakness or a character flaw. It's what happens to people who *have* a conscience and were put in impossible situations, often young, often with no good options. Many veterans live with this quietly for years. The fact that it still hurts is a sign of how much you cared — not proof that you're beyond repair.

I'm a companion here to sit with you and think this through, not a therapist or doctor. I won't tell you what's wrong with you or hand you a diagnosis. What I can do is stay steady with you and point toward real help.

## A few things you can actually try

- **Name it out loud, plainly.** Shame grows in silence. Try finishing this sentence, even just to yourself or to me: "The part I can't put down is ___." Getting it into words — not fixing it, just naming it — often loosens its grip a little.
- **Separate the act from the whole of you.** Guilt says *"I did something I regret."* Shame says *"I am bad."* Those are different. You can hold responsibility for a moment in your past without letting it define your entire worth. Ask yourself: would you judge a battle buddy this harshly for the same thing?
- **Ground yourself when the memory floods in.** Try **5-4-3-2-1**: name 5 things you see, 4 you can touch, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste. Or **box breathing** — in for 4, hold 4, out for 4, hold 4. This tells your body you're here, now, safe.
- **Do one small thing that reflects your values.** Moral injury heals partly through living out who you want to be — helping another vet, being honest, showing up for family. Small acts of decency, repeated, rebuild self-respect over time.
- **Protect your sleep.** Guilt and shame get louder when you're exhausted. Keep a steady wake-up time, cut screens and caffeine late, and get morning light. It won't erase the pain, but a rested mind fights fairer.

## When to reach out for real support

You don't have to carry this alone, and talking to a real human is a sign of strength, not failure. Consider reaching out if the guilt or shame is constant, if you're isolating, drinking or using more, or if you've had thoughts that life isn't worth living.

- **Veterans Crisis Line:** Dial **988, then press 1** — or text **838255**, or chat online. It's free, confidential, and staffed 24/7, many by fellow veterans.
- Ask your **VA provider or Vet Center** about moral injury groups and evidence-based therapies. The VA has clinicians who work with this specifically.
- Chaplains, peer specialists, and trusted people in your life can walk alongside you too.

Reaching out today doesn't fix everything at once. It's just the next right step — and you don't have to take it by yourself.
