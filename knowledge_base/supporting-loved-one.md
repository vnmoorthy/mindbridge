# For Family: Supporting a Veteran You Love

## What's happening, and why it's common

When someone comes home from service, the war doesn't always stay behind them. A veteran living with PTSD may pull away, sleep badly, startle easily, get irritable, or go quiet for stretches. This isn't a character flaw and it isn't about you. It's how a nervous system that learned to survive danger keeps firing after the danger is gone. Millions of veterans carry this, and it's treatable. You don't have to fix it, and you can't. What you *can* do is be a steady, safe presence while they find real help. That matters more than most people realize.

This companion isn't a therapist or a doctor. Think of it as a knowledgeable friend who can point you toward things that help.

## Things you can actually try

- **Listen more than you advise.** When they talk, resist the urge to solve it or say "at least." Try: "That sounds heavy. I'm here." Feeling heard lowers the pressure. Silence together is okay too.
- **Learn the flashback signs and stay grounded.** If they seem far away, panicked, or "back there," speak calmly, use their name, and remind them where they are: the date, the room, that they're safe now. You can offer a simple grounding exercise called **5-4-3-2-1** — name 5 things you see, 4 you can touch, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste. It pulls attention back to the present.
- **Breathe with them, don't at them.** If they're willing, do slow **box breathing** side by side: in for 4, hold 4, out for 4, hold 4. Doing it together feels less like a command.
- **Protect the small stuff: sleep and routine.** Nights are often the hardest. Support steady sleep habits — consistent bedtime, less caffeine and alcohol late, a wind-down without screens. Predictable daily rhythms help a rattled nervous system feel safer.
- **Ask before touch and respect their space.** A sudden hand on the shoulder can trigger a startle. Ask, "Is a hug okay right now?" Letting them keep some control is itself calming.
- **Take care of yourself, too.** Supporting someone is draining. Your own rest, friendships, and breaks aren't selfish — they're what let you keep showing up.

## When to reach out for real support

You're a companion, not a rescue service — and some moments need trained humans.

If your veteran talks about ending their life, gives away belongings, or you feel they're in danger, don't wait. Call the **Veterans Crisis Line: dial 988, then press 1** (or text 838255, or chat at VeteransCrisisLine.net). It's free, confidential, 24/7, and works even if they're not enrolled in VA care.

For ongoing support, encourage professional and peer help — it works, and asking for it is strength, not weakness. Good starting points:

- **VA National Center for PTSD** (ptsd.va.gov/family) — plain-language guides and the free **PTSD Family Coach** app for loved ones.
- **VA Caregiver Support Line:** 1-855-260-3274.
- **NAMI Family-to-Family:** 1-800-950-6264 — free education for families.

You don't have to carry this alone, and neither do they.
