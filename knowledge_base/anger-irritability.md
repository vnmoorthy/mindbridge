# Anger, Irritability & the Startle Response

## What's happening, and why it's so common

If you find yourself snapping at people you love, feeling a slow burn of irritation all day, or jumping out of your skin at a slammed door or a car backfiring — you are not broken, and you are not alone. These are some of the most common experiences for veterans living with PTSD.

Here's the plain version of why. Downrange, your body learned to stay switched on. Fast anger and a sharp startle kept you and your buddies alive. The problem is the body doesn't automatically get the memo that you're home now. The alarm system stays cranked up, so it reads a crowded store, a tailgater, or a sudden noise as a threat — and floods you with the same fight-or-flight response you needed in combat. The anger and the jumpiness aren't character flaws. They're a nervous system that's still doing its old job a little too well.

This can be walked back. Not erased overnight, but softened, with practice and the right support.

## A few things you can actually try

- **Name it and buy 90 seconds.** When you feel the heat rising, tell yourself "this is the alarm, not the enemy." A surge of anger peaks and starts to fade in about a minute and a half if you don't feed it. Step outside, get a glass of water, walk to the truck — put space between the spark and the reaction.
- **Box breathing.** Breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 4, out for 4, hold for 4. Repeat four or five rounds. It's the same steadying tool used before you take a shot, and it tells your body the threat has passed.
- **5-4-3-2-1 grounding when you're startled or on edge.** Name 5 things you see, 4 you can touch, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste. It pulls you out of the alarm and back into the room you're actually standing in.
- **Guard your sleep.** Short, wrecked sleep is gasoline on irritability. Aim for a consistent wake-up time, cut caffeine after midday, and keep the phone out of the bed. Even small wins here take the edge off.
- **Burn off the charge.** Anger is energy. Rucking, lifting, a hard walk, or splitting wood gives that fight-or-flight fuel somewhere to go instead of landing on the people around you.
- **Give people a heads-up.** Tell someone you trust, "When I go quiet or short, I'm not mad at you — I need a few minutes." It turns a fight into a plan.

## When to reach out for real support

I'm a companion, not a therapist — and the good news is that anger and hyperarousal respond really well to real help. Proven approaches like CBT and prolonged exposure work with a professional to turn the alarm down at the source. Reach out if the anger is scaring you or the people you love, if it's costing you relationships or work, or if you're using alcohol or anything else to take the edge off.

The Veterans Crisis Line is free, confidential, and staffed 24/7 by people who get it: **dial 988 then press 1**, text **838255**, or chat online at [VeteransCrisisLine.net](https://www.veteranscrisisline.net/). You can also connect with a VA provider or a peer support specialist — a fellow veteran who's walked this road. Reaching out is the strong move, not the weak one.
