# Sleep, Nightmares & Rest

## What's happening, and why it's so common

If you're lying awake at 3 a.m., or jolting up from the same dream, or sleeping with the lights on and one eye open — you're not broken, and you're not alone. Trouble sleeping is one of the most common things veterans living with PTSD deal with. Your body spent a long time learning to stay alert to survive. That wiring doesn't just switch off when you get home. Nightmares, waking up on edge, dreading the bed, or running on a few hours of sleep for years — these are normal responses to what you've carried, not a sign of weakness.

None of this means something is wrong with you. It means your nervous system is still doing its job a little too well. The good news: sleep is one of the areas where small, steady changes can genuinely help, and real support is out there.

## A few things you can actually try

- **Slow your breathing to signal "safe."** Try box breathing: breathe in for 4, hold for 4, out for 4, hold for 4. Repeat five or six rounds. Longer exhales tell your body the threat is over. Good before bed, or after a nightmare.
- **Come back to right now with 5-4-3-2-1.** If you wake up rattled, name 5 things you can see, 4 you can hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste. This grounding tool pulls you out of the dream and back into the room you're safe in.
- **Get out of bed if sleep won't come.** If you're still wide awake after about 20 minutes, get up, keep the lights low, and do something calm until you feel drowsy. This keeps your brain from learning that bed = lying there frustrated.
- **Build a wind-down runway.** Same rough bedtime and wake time daily, even weekends. Cut screens, caffeine, and heavy scrolling in the last hour. Keep the room cool and dark. Small anchors add up.
- **Don't fight the nightmare alone.** A well-known approach called imagery rehearsal has people gently rewrite the ending of a recurring dream while awake, so it loses some of its grip. Many veterans find this genuinely helps — and it works best with a trained counselor walking you through it.
- **Ease the after-midnight dread.** Keep a glass of water, a light, or a familiar object within reach so waking up feels less alone. A short walk, journaling, or a call to a battle buddy can reset a rough night.

## When to reach out for real support

I'm a companion, not a doctor or therapist — and some things deserve real human help. If nightmares or sleeplessness are wearing you down, the VA offers proven treatments like CBT-I (therapy for insomnia), which improves sleep for most people who complete it. Talking to your VA provider, a Vet Center, or a peer specialist is a strong, not a weak, move.

And if a night ever gets dark — if you're thinking about hurting yourself or just can't hold on — reach out now. The **Veterans Crisis Line** is free, confidential, and open 24/7: **dial 988 then press 1**, text **838255**, or chat at VeteransCrisisLine.net. You don't need to be enrolled in VA care to use it. You don't have to white-knuckle this alone.
