# Alcohol, Substances & Healthier Coping

## What's happening, and why it's so common

If you're leaning on alcohol or other substances to get through the day or the night, you are not weak and you are not alone. A lot of veterans living with PTSD end up doing the same thing. There's even a name for the pattern experts use — "self-medicating." When sleep won't come, when the memories crowd in, when everything feels too loud, a drink or a pill can seem like the one thing that turns the volume down. It works for a little while — that's exactly why it's so easy to reach for.

The hard part is what comes after. Alcohol and many substances actually worsen the very things they seem to quiet — they wreck sleep, crank up anxiety and anger the next day, and can pull you into a cycle that's tough to climb out of alone. Noticing that pattern isn't failure. It's the first honest step, and it takes real guts to look at it.

I'm an AI companion, not a doctor or counselor, so I can't diagnose anything or tell you to change a medication. What I can do is sit with you and share a few things other veterans have found useful.

## A few things you can actually try

- **Ride the urge instead of fighting it.** Cravings feel permanent but usually crest and fade in about 15–20 minutes, like a wave. When one hits, set a timer, get a cold glass of water, and just notice the urge without acting on it. This is sometimes called "urge surfing." You don't have to win the war tonight — just outlast this one wave.

- **Ground yourself with 5-4-3-2-1.** When your head is spinning, name 5 things you can see, 4 you can hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you can smell, and 1 you can taste. It pulls your brain out of the past and back into this room, right now — often the moment you'd otherwise reach for something.

- **Steady your breathing.** Try box breathing: in for 4 counts, hold for 4, out for 4, hold for 4. A few rounds tells your nervous system the threat has passed. It's free, it's silent, and no one around you even knows you're doing it.

- **Guard your sleep without the bottle.** Alcohol knocks you out but shreds the deep sleep PTSD already steals. Try a wind-down routine instead — same bedtime, screens off early, lights low, a cooler room. Rough nights are normal at first; give it a couple of weeks.

- **Delay and swap, don't just deny.** Tell yourself "not right now, in an hour" and fill that hour — a walk, a call to a buddy, push-ups, a shower. Line up a substitute you can grab before the urge shows up, not during.

## When to reach out for real support

These tools can steady a hard moment, but they aren't a substitute for real, human help — and you deserve that help. The VA treats substance use every day without judgment, and you can walk into any VA medical center **regardless of your discharge status or whether you're enrolled.** Call **800-827-1000** to find a program near you.

If you're thinking about ending your life, if you're mixing substances in ways that scare you, or if you just need someone right now, reach the **Veterans Crisis Line: dial 988 then press 1, text 838255, or chat online.** It's free, confidential, and staffed 24/7 — many responders are veterans themselves. Reaching out is the strong move. I'm glad you're still here.
