# Reconnecting & Rebuilding Camaraderie

## What's happening, and why it's so common

In the service, you were never alone. You had a unit, a shared mission, people who had your back around the clock, and a language everyone understood without explaining. Then it ends. Civilian life can feel quiet in a way that's loud — small talk feels hollow, folks "don't get it," and the tight bond you counted on just isn't there anymore.

If you're feeling isolated, like an outsider, or like nobody could ever understand what you carry, know this: that feeling is one of the most common things veterans describe. It isn't weakness or a flaw. It's grief for something real you lost — that brotherhood and sisterhood was worth missing. The good news is that connection can be rebuilt. It usually starts smaller than you'd think.

## Things you can actually try

- **Reach out to one person — just one.** You don't have to rebuild your whole network today. Text a battle buddy something simple: "Been thinking about you. How you holding up?" One low-stakes message can reopen a door. If they don't answer right away, it's not a rejection — try another name on the list.

- **Build shoulder-to-shoulder, not face-to-face.** A lot of camaraderie was forged doing things side by side, not sitting across a table talking. Find a shared activity — a gym session, a range day, a project, a rec league, a volunteer crew. Doing something together takes the pressure off the words.

- **Find spaces built for veterans.** VFW and American Legion posts, Team Red White & Blue, and VA peer support specialists connect you with people who share the same language and experiences. VA peer specialists are veterans themselves — folks who've walked a similar road.

- **Steady yourself before you go in.** If walking into a room full of people feels like too much, ground first. Try 5-4-3-2-1: name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you smell, 1 you taste. Or box breathing — in for 4, hold 4, out for 4, hold 4. It settles the body so the moment feels manageable.

- **Reconnect through purpose.** Many veterans find their way back to belonging through a mission — mentoring a younger vet, volunteering, coaching, service work. Purpose has a way of putting good people around you.

- **Say it plainly to the people who love you.** Instead of pulling away, try telling family: "I miss having my unit around me." Naming it lets them stand with you instead of guessing.

## When to reach out for real support

I'm a companion, not a therapist or a crisis service — and some things deserve real human help. If the isolation is deepening, if you feel hopeless, or if you're having thoughts of not being here anymore, please reach out now. The **Veterans Crisis Line** is free, confidential, and open 24/7 — **dial 988 then press 1**, **text 838255**, or chat online. You don't need to be enrolled in VA benefits to use it. A VA provider or peer support specialist can also help you build connection over time. Reaching out is what strong people do.
